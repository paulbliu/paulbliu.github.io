## Basin-by-Basin Interpretation

## 1. Delaware Basin
Represented by:
- [[SILVERTIP 76-16 UNIT Q 2H - DCA Comparison]]
- [[CHERRY STATE 56-2-13-1 E 31H - Gas DCA Comparison]]

### What Delaware cases show
The Delaware examples illustrate:
- high-productivity unconventional wells
- strong early transient decline
- modern methods outperforming unconstrained Arps
- large stream volumes
- importance of distinguishing oil-led vs gas-rich behavior

### Key Delaware lessons
- high-rate wells can still produce misleading b > 1 if fit blindly
- gas-rich Delaware wells deserve stream-specific analysis
- modern methods often converge well when history is decent

---

## 2. Midland Basin
Represented by:
- [[SOUTH CURTIS RANCH 401SP - DCA Comparison]]

### What the Midland case shows
This case is especially valuable because it demonstrates:
- operational noise
- workovers / shut-ins
- partial-month distortions
- late-life trend interpretation
- how real field data can differ from textbook decline curves

### Key Midland lessons
- QC is not optional
- fit window definition strongly affects results
- longer history helps reveal BDF tendencies
- noisy data widens method spread and uncertainty

---

## 3. DJ Basin
Represented by:
- [[PROWANT 18-11HZ NBRR - DJ Basin DCA Comparison]]

### What the DJ case shows
This case provides the most important contrast to the Permian examples:
- lower water burden
- more moderate oil-rate scale
- cleaner mature profile
- different basin character despite still being an unconventional horizontal

### Key DJ lessons
- not all unconventional wells should be interpreted through a Permian mental model
- lower water burden can simplify late-life economics and decline interpretation
- cross-basin context matters when comparing EURs, rates, and method behavior

## Cross-Basin Lessons

### 1. “Unconventional” is not one behavior
Even though all four wells are unconventional horizontals, they behave differently because of:
- reservoir quality
- fluid mix
- basin-specific completion context
- water burden
- operational history

### 2. Method selection is only part of the story
The decline method matters, but these case studies show that:
- data quality matters
- stream choice matters
- basin context matters
- economic assumptions matter

### 3. Water burden changes the character of the case
This is one of the biggest cross-basin insights:
- Permian wells often force you to think harder about water burden
- the DJ case shows how a lower-water well can look and feel different operationally and economically

### 4. Stream-level thinking becomes more important as fluid mix changes
- SILVERTIP and South Curtis Ranch are mainly oil-led
- CHERRY STATE is much more gas-driven
- PROWANT is a useful lower-water oil contrast

This reinforces the importance of:
- [[Oil vs Gas vs BOE Decline Analysis]]

## Bottom Line
The case library now demonstrates four important truths:

1. **Modern DCA methods are more reliable than unconstrained Arps for unconventional wells**
2. **Data quality and QC decisions materially affect forecasting confidence**
3. **Stream choice (oil vs gas) changes interpretation**
4. **Basin context matters — not all unconventional wells behave like Permian wells**

That makes the vault much more than a collection of formulas — it is becoming a practical engineering decision system.
